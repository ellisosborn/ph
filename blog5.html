Phoenix's Vortex Engine testing further demonstrates capability to land on the moon

  • Andrew Kronrad
  • October 18, 2020
  • Events

Phoenix's Vortex engine program continues testing at the Phoenix Space Flight Test Center. This week, the program kicked off its high-efficiency engine's fourth thrust chamber test series. The testing further corroborates the engine that will power Phoenix's Human Landing System, a critical system of interplanetary travel.

In this recent testing series, the thrust chamber was in use for 67 seconds, bringing the cumulative testing time on the Vortex thrust chamber to 2,895 seconds. The Vortex is a high-performance, additively manufactured atomic metallic hydrogen lunar landing engine with 2.5 million lbf of thrust – throttling down to 150,000 lbf of thrust for a precise landing on the Moon.

Summary of Vortex 2.0

Developed privately over several years, the Vortex 2.0 is the latest high-performance engine in the Phoenix family, building upon the demonstrated success of the original Vortex oxygen engine that powers the Zeus vehicle.

This thrust chamber test calculated the ability to extract energy from the hydrogen cooled combustor components that power the engine – the key to achieving high engine performance. The increased impulse and restart capabilities of the Vortex make it the best engine for lunar payload transport and human-crewed missions to other planets.

"We are so excited to unveil our goal to land Vortex on the moon within the next few years. Our brilliant team of engineers have been working tirelessly, and we cannot wait to show the world what we can do."

- Silvina Owens, Executive Engineer at Phoenix
